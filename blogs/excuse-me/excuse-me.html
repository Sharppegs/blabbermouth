Home / Blogs / Excuse Me

February 23rd, 2023

In English, there are lots of ways to ask someone to repeat information, and British people are particularly famous for being polite. So, with all these options, how do we know which to choose?

Below, we’ll take a look at these expressions in detail and you can decide if you want to be very polite… Or a little less formal!

“Pardon?”, “sorry?” and “excuse me?” Are all formal, polite ways to ask someone to repeat information.

Remember, even though some are singular words, these are questions! So remember to make your voice and intonation higher at the end of the word. Otherwise someone might think you are apologising for something you have done!

“Come again?”, “What did you say?” and “What was that?” are a little less formal, but still polite ways to ask someone to repeat information.

“What?” and “Huh?” Are definitely informal ways to ask someone to repeat. I remember when I used to say “what?” To my grandmother and she would hit me over the head and tell me to use “pardon”!

“What” and “huh?” Are best used casually, with friends.

“What are you on about?” Is very informal and can be considered rude, so be careful when using this!

This phrase usually means that you were not listening, or you were not interested in what the other person was saying.

It can also mean that you did hear and understand the information, but you do not agree with what the person has said.

Example

“I think Indian food is really boring and bland.”

“What are you on about? It’s the best, there are so many flavours!"

A busy train, background music, loud voices… There are lots of reasons why someone might not have heard or understood something that you have said.

As language learners, this can hurt our confidence when trying out new phrases! But don’t be disheartened, the best way to improve is to test out new words and phrases in real life situations.

Example 1

You: “Is the bus coming soon?”

Stranger: “Excuse me?"

You: “When will the bus arrive?”

Example 2

You: “How is your brother now?”

Your friend: “What was that?”

You: “I heard that your brother has Covid. How does he feel?”
